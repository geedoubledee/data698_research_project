Initial Data Preparation for Unsupervised Learning

While some positive reviews may identify problems with a recipe, it is reasonable to assume that negative reviews are more likely to do so. So we first create a corpus of potentially negative review text. This will make manually labeling reviews by what problem they are identifying faster later by hopefully limiting the number of reviews we will have to look at that dont identify any problems at all. We define a potentially negative review as any text review combined with a star rating of 3 or less.

We pivot our wide review data into a longer format, dropping some columns we dont need for this stage of the analysis.

Unsupervised Learning: Topic Modeling

We start by fuzzy clustering the reviews into topics using bigrams. Since some words that are commonly considered stopwords for text data might be useful for our analysis, we will not exclude bigrams that contain words like too and not from consideration. Many reviews will definitely include text like too much or not enough, and we hope fuzzy clustering will group reviews together based on their similar language. We also eliminate bigrams containing numbers.

stop_w <- as.data.frame(stopwords(language = "en", source = "nltk"))

Using values of k from two to 12, we measure the perplexity of several different Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models that would automatically divide the data into k topics.

get_perplexity <- function(dtm, k_max){
    #function adapted from https://bit.ly/4cLUHO8
    m <- matrix(,1,1)
    for (i in 2:k_max){
        lda <- LDA(dtm, i)
        per <- perplexity(lda)
        m <- plyr::rbind.fill.matrix(m, per)
    }
    m <- as.data.frame(cbind(matrix(seq(2, k_max), ncol = 1), m[-1,]))
    colnames(m) <- c("topics", "perplexity")
    return(m)
}

We see a lot of overlap in the bigrams that are common to these six topics. In topics one, two, and six, next time is the most identifying bigram, indicating the reviews in each of these topics are likely to exhibit some disappointment in the results and some suggestions for improving those results in the future. Despite all the overlap, there are also bigrams that are unique to each topic.

Its notable that some of these unique bigrams are related to specific ingredients, i.e.brown sugar being an identifier of topic one, cream cheese being an identifier of topic four, and soy sauce being an identifier of topic six. Ingredients have therefore resulted in some unintended grouping. We check for an additional instance of unintended grouping since the reviews are of various lengths, and it is reasonable to suspect review length might have incidentally factored into the topic decision boundaries.
